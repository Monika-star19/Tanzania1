Visa

For traveling to Tanzania, you will need a valid visa, which can be obtained online, at Tanzanian embassies/consulates,
or on arrival for certain nationalities. Types of visas include tourist, business, transit, student, and volunteer.

    Application Process
  • Online Application: Available through the Tanzania Immigration website.
  • Embassy/Consulate: Apply at the nearest Tanzanian embassy or consulate.
  • On Arrival: Visas are available on arrival for certain nationalities at major entry points
  • Visa Extensions: Available through the Tanzanian Immigration office if you need to extend your stay.
  • Yellow Fever Vaccination: Required if you are traveling from a country with a risk of yellow fever.

Health

  • Malaria, including chloroquine-resistant strains, occurs year-round, except in areas above 1800 metres.
    Consider taking anti-malarial medication.
  • Yellow fever can occur. Check with a health professional before travelling if you need to get vaccinated.
    Other insect-borne diseases include Zika virus, dengue, filariasis and East African trypanosomiasis (sleeping sickness).
    Ensure your accommodation is insect-proof and use insect repellent.
  • HIV/AIDS is widespread. Take precautions if you're taking part in high-risk activities.
  • Altitude sickness can affect anyone at heights over 2500 metres. If you plan to climb Mt Kilimanjaro (5895 metres),
    make sure you're physically fit and in good health. Talk to your doctor before you travel.
  • Medical facilities are limited, and medicines are often not available. If you get injured or become ill, you may need to be
    evacuated to another country for treatment. This can be expensive. In the case of a medical emergency while in Tanzania,
    call the toll-free Health Emergency Number: 112.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Tanzania depends on what you want to experience:
  • Wildlife Viewing: The best time for wildlife viewing in Tanzania's national parks, like the Serengeti and Ngorongoro Crater,
    is during the dry season from late June to October. This is when animals gather around water sources, making them easier to spot.
  • The Great Migration: To witness the Great Migration, plan your visit between June and September. The wildebeest and zebra herds
    are in the northern Serengeti, and the river crossings are particularly dramatic.
  • Climbing Mount Kilimanjaro: The best months for climbing Kilimanjaro are January to March and June to October, when the weather is
    relatively stable.
  • Beach Holidays: For a beach holiday on Zanzibar, the best time is from June to October when the weather is warm and sunny.
    December to February is also a good time, but it can be hotter.
  • Avoiding the Rainy Seasons: Tanzania has two rainy seasons: the long rains from March to May and the short rains in November and December.
    Travel during these periods can be challenging due to muddy roads and disrupted wildlife viewing.

Currency and Payments

  • The official currency of Tanzania is the Tanzanian Shilling (TZS).
  • US dollars (USD) are widely accepted, especially in tourist areas, hotels, and for safaris.
  • Carry a mix of Tanzanian Shillings and US dollars for convenience.
  • Small denominations (USD 1, 5, 10, 20) are useful for tips and minor expenses.
  • Exchange services are available at airports, banks, and exchange bureaus in major cities.

Local Etiquette

  • Greetings are very important in Tanzanian culture. A handshake is the most common form of greeting,
    often accompanied by asking about the other person's health and family.
    Use the right hand for handshakes and passing items, as the left hand is considered unclean.
  • Dress modestly, especially in rural areas and Zanzibar, where the population is predominantly Muslim.
    Women should avoid wearing shorts and sleeveless tops outside of beach areas and resorts.
    Long skirts or trousers and covered shoulders are recommended. Men should also avoid wearing shorts
    in towns and villages.
  • Show respect to elders by addressing them formally and using titles where appropriate.
    Allow elders to speak first and listen attentively.
  • Always ask for permission before taking photos of people, especially in rural areas and Maasai villages.
    It is illegal to photograph military and government buildings, airports, and border crossings.
  • Gifts are appreciated but not expected. If giving a gift, choose something modest and appropriate.
    Avoid giving alcohol or pork products to Muslims.
  • Tanzanians often communicate indirectly to avoid confrontation. Be mindful of non-verbal cues and tone of voice.
    Avoid sensitive topics like politics, religion, and ethnic issues unless you know the person well.
  • Respect religious customs and practices. Dress appropriately when visiting religious sites.
    During Ramadan, avoid eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight hours in predominantly Muslim areas.
  • Do not litter and respect local efforts to keep areas clean. When visiting wildlife areas or national parks,
    follow the guidelines and respect the natural habitat.

Transportation

  • Major international airports include Julius Nyerere International Airport
    (Dar es Salaam), Kilimanjaro International Airport (near Arusha), and Abeid Amani Karume International Airport (Zanzibar).
  • Buses are a common and affordable way to travel between cities and towns.
  • Trains are a scenic but slower travel option, suitable for adventurous travelers.
  • Taxis are available in major cities and towns. Negotiate fares before starting your journey.
  • Car rental services are available in major cities and airports.

Communication

  • Swahili (Kiswahili): The national language spoken by the majority of Tanzanians and used in most daily interactions,
    government, and media.
  • Mobile phones are the primary means of communication, with a high penetration rate across the country.
    Major mobile network operators include Vodacom, Airtel, Tigo, and Halotel.
  • Internet access is widespread in urban areas, with 3G, 4G, and even 5G networks available.
    Rural areas may have limited or no internet access.
  • Start conversations with polite greetings. A handshake and "Habari" (How are you?) or "Shikamoo"
    (a respectful greeting to elders) are common.

Packing Essentials

  • Pack light, breathable clothing for hot weather. Cotton and linen fabrics are ideal.
  • Hiking Boots: If you plan to climb Mount Kilimanjaro or go trekking.
  • Basic first aid supplies including band-aids, antiseptic wipes, and any personal medications.
  • Non-perishable snacks for long journeys or when exploring remote areas.
